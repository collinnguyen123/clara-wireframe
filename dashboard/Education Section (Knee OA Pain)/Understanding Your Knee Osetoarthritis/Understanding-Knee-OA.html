Understanding your knee osteoarthritis

A plain language guide to what is happening in your knee, why it hurts, and what actually helps.

From your Clara care team 15 min read Checked against current guidelines
Knee osteoarthritis is one of the most common causes of joint pain, and there is a lot you can do about it.

Start here: the one thing to remember

If you take away a single idea from this guide, let it be this: movement is the most powerful treatment we have for knee osteoarthritis. Not rest. Not avoiding the stairs. Gentle, regular movement. The rest of this guide explains what your knee is doing, why it hurts, and the small, doable things that make the biggest difference. Read it once, then keep it nearby.

What knee osteoarthritis actually is

Osteoarthritis (we will call it OA) is the most common form of arthritis. In a healthy knee, the ends of your bones are capped with cartilage, a smooth, slippery cushion that lets the joint glide and absorb shock.

In OA, that cushion gradually thins, the joint lining can become mildly inflamed, small bony bumps called spurs may form around the edges, and the muscles around the knee often get weaker.

The important part: OA is a whole joint condition, not just a worn out cushion. That means there are many places to help, the muscles, the inflammation, your weight, your activity, not just the cartilage. It is why the right plan can make a real difference even though cartilage does not grow back.(1)

Why it happens, and who tends to get it

OA usually comes from a mix of things adding up over time, not one single cause. The most common contributors are:

  • Age and genetics. Risk rises with age, and OA often runs in families.
  • Being female. For reasons not fully understood, women are more likely to develop it.
  • Extra body weight. More weight means more load on the knee, and body fat also releases signals that add to joint inflammation.
  • Past knee injuries. A previous significant injury, like an ACL tear or fracture, raises the risk in that knee.
  • Certain jobs and postures. Work with a lot of kneeling, squatting, or heavy lifting can contribute.
  • Muscle weakness. Weak thigh and hip muscles leave the knee less supported.

And one reassuring point worth repeating: normal recreational activity, including regular walking, does NOT cause knee OA, and does not wear your knee out. Studies of everyday and recreational exercisers, even runners, do not show added damage. Staying active protects your knee(4).

Why your knee hurts

This surprises a lot of people: the cartilage itself has no nerves. So OA pain is not the cartilage ‘rubbing’ or ‘grinding.’ The pain actually comes from the parts of the joint that do have nerves: the bone just beneath the cartilage, the inflamed joint lining, and the muscles and tissues around the knee.

That also explains something patients often notice: pain and X-rays do not always match. Some people with only mild changes on imaging hurt quite a bit, and some with larger changes barely notice. Your pain is real, and it is driven by more than the picture(3). Over time, if pain has been around a while, the nervous system itself can become more sensitive, which is one reason a calm, steady plan beats chasing the pain day to day.

Busting the biggest myth: “wear and tear”

The phrase “wear and tear” is one of the oldest and most harmful ideas in joint care, because it makes people afraid to move. The truth is the opposite: people who move less tend to have more pain, more stiffness, and more loss of function over time.

The reassuring evidence

Low impact exercise does not damage your cartilage on imaging. Moving does not 'use up' your knee.

Walking up to about 10,000 steps a day has not been shown to speed up OA(4).

If you already enjoy an activity, you very likely do not need to give it up. We help you adjust it, not abandon it.

What actually helps: the four truths

These four ideas are the foundation of your Clara plan. They are backed by every major guideline.

1. Movement is medicine

In the largest review to date (139 trials), exercise eased knee pain about as much as taking an oral anti-inflammatory plus acetaminophen together, and unlike a pill, the benefit kept going for 2 to 6 months after a program ended(4). You do not need a gym. Walking, cycling, swimming, or a gentle class all count.

2. Stronger muscles mean a stronger knee

Your thigh (quadriceps) and hip muscles are the knee's shock absorbers. When they get stronger, the joint carries less load and pain drops. Consistency matters far more than lifting heavy, gentle and regular wins.

3. Weight matters, and the math is on your side

Every 1 pound you lose takes about 4 pounds of pressure off your knee with each step(6, 7). A 5 to 10 percent weight reduction over a few months produces meaningful pain relief on its own, and it also lowers the inflammation that body fat adds to the joint(6, 7).

4. Understanding your condition is itself a treatment

This is not a throwaway line. In studies, education and a clear plan reduce pain on their own(6). Knowing what is happening, and what to do, lowers fear, and fear of movement is a big driver of disability in OA.

Your movement menu (pick one main lane you will actually keep doing, then add a second):

  • Aerobic: walking, stationary cycling, swimming, or water walking. The most studied and the top performer overall.
  • Strengthening: simple thigh and hip exercises, 3 to 4 times a week. Start with body weight, build slowly.
  • Tai chi or yoga: tai chi works as well as physical therapy for many people, and it helps mood and sleep too(5).
  • Water exercise: the pool carries most of your weight, ideal if land exercise hurts or during a flare.

Where medicines fit in

Medicines come second. They support your movement plan, they do not replace it. When they help, here is the general order:

  • Topical anti-inflammatory creams come first. They work right where it hurts, with very little reaching the rest of the body, so they are gentle and well tolerated. This is the foundation of Clara's compound cream(6, 7).
  • Targeted oral options come next when more help is needed, chosen and reviewed by your doctor for your situation.
  • Injections and other tools exist for specific situations and flares, and your care team will guide you if they fit.

Modern, non-opioid care

Clara does not use opioids in any of our treatments. Opioids carry real risks and do not improve knee OA in the long run(6, 7). Everything in your plan is built around safer, modern, non-opioid options.

What does not help as much as people hope

So you can spend your time and money where it counts:

  • Rest as a strategy. Short rest during a bad flare is fine, but resting your way out of OA backfires.
  • Glucosamine and chondroitin supplements. The evidence is inconsistent, and quality varies a lot between brands(6, 7).
  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol) alone. It offers only small benefit for knee OA, though it can have a limited role for some people(6, 7).
  • Wedge shoe inserts. Trials have not shown a clear benefit for most people(6, 8).

None of these are dangerous in normal use. They just are not where the real gains are.

What to expect over time

OA is often imagined as a steady downhill slide. The research is more hopeful than that. In studies that followed people for years, most stayed about the same, and changes on X-ray happen slowly(3). Pain tends to come and go, you will have better weeks and harder weeks, and flares settle. A consistent plan keeps you ahead of it, and many people stay active and comfortable for decades.

When to check in with your own doctor, or consider getting care

  • A knee that suddenly becomes hot, red, or very swollen, or if you develop a fever.
  • Not being able to put weight on the knee after a fall or injury, or the knee locking or giving way.
  • Pain or swelling in your calf.
  • Pain that keeps getting worse week after week despite following your plan.
  • New numbness, tingling, or weakness in the leg.

If any of these happen, please see your family doctor or an urgent clinic.

A knee OA diagnosis is not a sentence to slow down. It is an invitation to move in a smarter, kinder way, with a team beside you. Small, steady steps, the walk you take, the exercises you keep up, the weight you ease off, add up to real relief. We will be with you the whole way.
