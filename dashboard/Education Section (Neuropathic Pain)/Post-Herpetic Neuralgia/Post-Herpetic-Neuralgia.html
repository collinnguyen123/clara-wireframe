Nerve pain · Post-herpetic neuralgia

Pain after shingles

When shingles clears but the pain stays, the nerve itself has been hurt. Here is what is happening, what your doctor can do, and why this is the one nerve-pain condition where a cream is best proven.

What it is

Shingles is a painful, blistering rash caused by the chickenpox virus reactivating in the body. For most people the rash heals on its own within about two to four weeks.(1)

In some people the pain does not leave with the rash. When burning or shooting nerve pain lasts three months or more in the same band of skin where the shingles was, it is called post-herpetic neuralgia, or PHN. It is the most common lasting complication of shingles, and the skin over it can become so sensitive that light touch, clothing or even a breeze can hurt.(1, 2)

Where the pain comes from

The chickenpox virus never fully leaves the body. After childhood chickenpox it lies quietly for decades inside the nerve roots near your spine. When age, illness or stress lowers your immune defences, the virus can wake up, travel back down a nerve to the skin, and cause the shingles rash.(2)

During that flare-up, the virus can injure the nerve fibres themselves. Damaged nerves do not simply stop working; they misfire, sending strong, repeated pain signals to the brain even when nothing is touching the skin. That faulty signalling is why the pain can outlast the rash by months, and it is also why ordinary painkillers, which are built for ordinary aches, often do not help much.(2)

The single biggest factor is age. The risk of lingering nerve pain after shingles climbs steeply the older you are, which is why PHN is uncommon in young adults but far more likely after 60.(2)

Other things that raise the risk include a weakened immune system, diabetes, severe pain or a severe rash during the shingles attack, and shingles that affects the face or eye. Having strong pain before the rash even appears can also be a warning sign.(2)

There is good news in the long run: for most people the pain of PHN slowly eases over months, though for some it can last a year or longer.(1)

What it feels like

PHN pain stays in one band of skin, following the path of the affected nerve. It is most common across the chest and trunk, on the face, or around the neck. People usually describe two kinds of pain, often at the same time:(2)

A constant, burning ache
A steady, deep, burning or throbbing pain that is there in the background. The skin in that area may also feel numb or oddly dull.
Sudden, electric jolts
Brief, sharp, shooting or shock-like stabs that come and go on their own, or are set off by movement or touch.

A hallmark of PHN is that gentle, normally painless touch becomes painful, so a shirt, a bedsheet or a light breeze can trigger it. Doctors call this allodynia. Because the pain can be relentless, it commonly disrupts sleep, mood and everyday activities, which is part of why treating it matters.(2)

How it is diagnosed

PHN is diagnosed from your story and a physical exam, not from a scan or blood test. The picture is usually clear: nerve pain that has lasted three months or more, in the same area of skin where you had a shingles rash.(2)

Extra tests, such as an MRI, are only needed when something is unusual, for example pain that does not match a past rash, or added weakness or numbness that suggests another problem. For most people, no further testing is required.(2)

Can it be prevented

To a meaningful degree, yes. The best protection is the shingles vaccine, which lowers the chance of getting shingles in the first place and, if you do get it, lowers the chance of developing lasting nerve pain. Vaccination is generally recommended for adults over 50.(1)

If shingles does appear, starting an antiviral medicine within about 72 hours of the rash helps the rash heal faster and eases the pain of the attack. It may also lower the risk of lingering nerve pain afterward, though the evidence on that point is mixed. Either way, treating shingles early and getting pain under control quickly is worthwhile.(1, 3)

What a doctor may recommend

PHN is treated step by step, and plans are personalised. Treatment usually starts with a medicine taken by mouth and often adds a treatment applied to the skin. Which medicine comes first depends partly on whether your pain is more of the constant-burning type or the electric-shooting type.(2)

1
Nerve-calming medicines by mouth
The usual first choices are anti-seizure medicines such as gabapentin or pregabalin, or low-dose tricyclic antidepressants such as nortriptyline or amitriptyline. These are not used here for seizures or depression; at these doses they quiet overactive nerve signals. They are started low and increased slowly to limit side effects like drowsiness or dizziness.(2)
2
Treatments applied to the skin
Because PHN pain sits in one area, treatments placed directly over it are a natural fit. Lidocaine (over the counter at 4%, by prescription at 5%) and capsaicin, made from chili peppers, are both used over the painful skin, on their own or alongside a pill.(2)
3
Other options if needed
If the first medicines are not enough, a doctor may try a different class, such as duloxetine or venlafaxine, or use a short course of a medicine like tramadol while other treatments are adjusted. Talk, or behavioural, therapy can also genuinely help people cope with and reduce nerve pain.(2)
4
For stubborn, severe pain
When pain does not respond to two or more medicines, a pain specialist may offer a high-strength capsaicin patch applied in the clinic, nerve blocks or injections, or nerve-stimulation procedures. These are reserved for more difficult cases.(2)

How a compound cream can help

Strongest evidence for a cream

Of all the nerve-pain conditions, PHN is the one where treating the pain through the skin is best proven. The reason is simple: the pain stays in one defined area, so a cream can act right where it hurts while far less medicine reaches the rest of your body than a pill would deliver.(4)

Lidocaine is especially helpful for the touch-sensitive part of PHN. It calms the irritated nerve endings just under the skin and adds a thin protective layer, which can take the sting out of the allodynia, the pain set off by light touch.(4, 7) Capsaicin, from chili peppers, is another proven topical option; a high-strength version is approved specifically for PHN.(6)

A compound cream lets a pharmacist combine more than one of these active ingredients into a single preparation made for you. For PHN the best-studied combination pairs amitriptyline with ketamine, often together with lidocaine. In studies covering more than 700 people, this mixture eased pain about as well as a common oral nerve medicine (gabapentin), while very little of the drug was absorbed into the bloodstream.(5)

Why a cream can be a good fit

It works where the pain is
Applied over the affected band of skin, it targets the painful area directly instead of treating your whole body.(4)
Less reaches the rest of the body
Because little is absorbed into the bloodstream, creams can be a sensible choice for older adults or people already taking several medicines, who may not tolerate the drowsiness or dizziness of oral nerve medicines.(4, 8)

An honest word on the evidence

A cream usually takes the edge off rather than removing pain entirely, and the evidence is not all one way. One large trial of multi-ingredient pain creams found no benefit over placebo, and reviews note that studies of some topical ingredients are limited or mixed. Compound creams are also not FDA approved, because each one is mixed individually rather than mass-produced. The sensible approach is to give a cream a fair four-to-six-week trial as one part of a plan your clara doctor builds and monitors with you.(8, 9)

Using a nerve-pain cream safely

Never apply a compound cream to broken, cut, raw or burned skin, keep it well away from your eyes, do not cover it with tight dressings or external heat, and store it out of reach of children and pets. Mild stinging, warmth or redness where you apply it is common and usually settles. A clara doctor reviews every ingredient against your health, allergies and other medicines before anything is made.(8)
