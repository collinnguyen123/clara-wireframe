Diabetic nerve pain

Years of high blood sugar can quietly damage the nerves, usually starting in the feet. Here is what is happening, what your doctor can do, and how a cream may help the burning when the pain stays in one place.

From your clara care team⏱ 9 min readEvidence based

Diabetic neuropathy is nerve damage caused by diabetes. It is the most common long term effect of diabetes, and over the years it affects up to about half of everyone living with type 1 or type 2 diabetes.(1, 2)

Most often it is a slow, even loss of feeling that starts in the toes and feet and can creep up the legs and into the hands, sometimes called a "stocking and glove" pattern. For some people it is painless and shows up only as numbness; for others it brings a burning, distracting pain.(2)

When blood sugar runs high for a long time, it slowly damages nerves. The longest nerves, the ones that reach all the way to the feet, are hit first, which is why symptoms usually start in the toes and move upward.(1)

The tiny nerve fibres that carry pain and temperature are often injured early. As they are hurt they start to misfire, sending burning or stabbing signals to the brain even when nothing is wrong with the skin. An honest point: once nerves are damaged this way, no treatment reverses it. So the goals are to slow further harm, ease the pain, and protect the feet.(3)

The biggest factor is how high your blood sugar has run, and for how long. The risk grows the longer you have had diabetes, and it is generally more common in type 2.(1, 2)

Smoking, high blood pressure, high blood fats and carrying extra weight all add to the risk. The encouraging news is that several of these are things you and your care team can work on.(1)

Diabetic nerve pain almost always affects both sides and is noticed first in the toes and feet. People usually describe some mix of:(1)

Burning, tingling or "pins and needles"
A burning or prickling feeling in the feet, often with such tenderness that even the weight of a bedsheet feels unpleasant.
Numbness and loss of feeling
A dull or deadened feeling in the feet. This is the quiet danger, because losing sensation makes injuries easy to miss.

The pain is often worse at rest and at night, which can badly disrupt sleep. The numbness matters just as much: when you cannot feel a blister, cut or rub, a small injury can quietly grow into a serious foot wound. That is why daily foot care is so important.(1)

This is usually diagnosed from your history and a careful look at the feet, not a scan or special test. Your doctor checks whether you can feel light touch, vibration and temperature, and tests the reflex at the ankle. A soft nylon strand pressed against the skin checks whether you can still feel protective sensation.(1)

More involved tests, like nerve studies, are usually only needed when the picture is unusual. Because the condition can be silent, everyone with diabetes is advised to have their feet checked regularly.(1)

The most powerful thing that slows nerve damage is keeping blood sugar in a healthy range. In type 1 diabetes, tight control sharply lowers the risk. In type 2 the benefit of sugar control alone is smaller, so the plan also looks at blood pressure, cholesterol, weight and quitting smoking.(3)

Just as important is daily foot care: checking both feet every day (including between the toes), keeping skin clean and moisturised, never going barefoot, and wearing shoes that fit well. Catching a small problem early is what prevents a serious one.(1)

When nerve pain needs treating, it is built up step by step and tailored to you. Pain medicines help the pain but do not fix numbness, and the aim is to find an option that helps with side effects you can live with.(3)

1
Nerve calming medicines by mouth
Usually a first choice is an antidepressant that works on nerve pain (such as duloxetine, venlafaxine or a low dose of amitriptyline) or an anti-seizure medicine (pregabalin or gabapentin). Several are approved for diabetic nerve pain. Started low and raised slowly, with a fair trial of a couple of months.(3)
2
Treatments applied to the skin
Because the pain sits in the feet, treatments placed right there fit naturally. Capsaicin, from chili peppers, comes as a cream or a strong patch applied in clinic that is approved for diabetic foot pain, and lidocaine can be used over a painful area.(3)
3
Combining or switching
If one medicine is not enough, a doctor may switch to another kind or combine two that work in different ways. Each change is given a proper trial before deciding.(3)
4
What is usually avoided
Opioid painkillers are usually advised against for diabetic nerve pain, because the long term evidence is weak and the risks, including tolerance and dependence, are real. For pain that does not respond, a pain specialist may consider other approaches.(3)

Strong evidence for a cream

After shingles pain, diabetic nerve pain has some of the best evidence for treating nerve pain through the skin. When the pain is focused in the feet, a cream can act right where it hurts while far less medicine reaches the rest of your body than a pill would.(4)

A compound cream lets a pharmacist combine more than one proven ingredient into a single preparation made for you. In a head to head study, a cream with amitriptyline eased diabetic nerve pain about as well as capsaicin, with fewer skin side effects.(5) Adding ketamine to amitriptyline is the best studied compounded pairing for nerve pain and is barely absorbed into the body.(6) A newer single ingredient option, phenytoin on the skin, has eased diabetic foot pain for some people in early studies.(7)

The most sensible approach is simple: give a cream a fair four to six week trial as one part of a plan your clara doctor builds and monitors with you, alongside good blood sugar control and foot care. A cream usually takes the edge off rather than erasing pain entirely, and because each one is compounded for you it is not approved by the FDA, so results can vary from person to person. Used as one piece of a wider plan, it is a reasonable option to try.(8)

Using a nerve pain cream safely

Never apply it to broken skin, a cut, a blister or a foot wound, which matters especially in diabetes where skin can be fragile and slow to heal. Keep it away from your eyes, do not cover it with tight dressings or heat, and store it out of reach of children and pets. Mild stinging, warmth or redness is common and usually settles. A clara doctor reviews every ingredient against your health, allergies and other medicines first.(5)
