Neuropathic pain

Nerve pain, and how a cream can help

Nerve pain is its own kind of pain, with its own rules. For pain in one area, a compound cream can work right where it hurts, with far less medicine reaching the rest of your body.

From your clara care team ⏱ 7 min read Evidence based

Most everyday pain comes from a muscle, joint, or bone. Neuropathic pain is different: it comes from the nerves themselves, the body’s wiring, sending faulty or exaggerated signals. Because the nerves are what is misfiring, the pain feels different too. People most often describe it as burning, tingling or pins and needles, shooting, or like an electric shock.(1)

A telltale sign is pain from things that should not hurt at all, like a light touch, clothing brushing the skin, or a cool breeze. Common causes include diabetes, shingles, nerve compression, and nerve injury after surgery.(1, 2)

Why a cream for nerve pain

For pain that sits in one area, a topical cream has a real advantage over a pill: it puts the medicine where the pain is, so far less of it reaches your bloodstream, which usually means fewer whole-body side effects.(3) That makes a cream especially worth considering if you cannot tolerate oral nerve-pain medicines, take many other medications, or simply prefer to avoid the drowsiness and other effects that the oral versions can bring.

Relief right where you rub it in
clara’s nerve-pain cream combines agents that calm overactive nerves at the surface, such as amitriptyline and ketamine, alongside options like lidocaine. They work in the skin and nerves of the painful area rather than the whole body.(5, 6)
Less medicine through the body
Because it is absorbed mostly where you apply it, a topical carries a lower risk of the system-wide effects that come with oral medicines. A clara doctor still reviews every ingredient against your health and other medications first.(3)

An honest word on what a cream can and cannot do

Compounded creams are made for the individual and are not FDA approved, and the evidence is mixed: a large trial found multi-ingredient creams no better than a placebo for some patients, while guidelines and clinical experience still support them for the right person with localized pain.(4, 13) Expect a cream to take the edge off, often a partial reduction rather than zero pain, give it a fair trial of about four to six weeks, and treat it as one part of a plan your clara doctor builds with you.

Nerve pain conditions a cream may help

The strength of the evidence is different for each condition, so we show it honestly. A clara doctor decides whether a cream is a sensible fit for you.

Strongest evidence
Pain after shingles
Shingles can damage a nerve and leave behind burning or stabbing pain that lasts long after the rash heals, known as post-herpetic neuralgia.(7)
How a cream helps: this is where topicals are best proven. Lidocaine eases the touch-sensitive pain, and a compounded amitriptyline and ketamine cream is the most studied combination for it.(7, 6)
Strongest evidence
Diabetic nerve pain
Years of high blood sugar can damage nerves, usually starting in the feet, with burning or stabbing pain in the toes and soles.(10)
How a cream helps: high-strength capsaicin is approved for painful diabetic neuropathy of the feet,(8, 9) and topical amitriptyline has matched capsaicin with fewer side effects in trials.(10)
Some evidence
Nerve pain after surgery or injury
Sometimes pain lingers in a scar or limb for months after an operation or injury, including nerve pain from cancer treatment.(12)
How a cream helps: for focused, well-defined pain, lidocaine, capsaicin and compounded amitriptyline, ketamine and lidocaine are used, and cancer-care guidelines list compounded creams as an option.(12)
Limited evidence
Complex regional pain syndrome
After an injury or surgery, a limb can develop pain out of proportion to the original problem, with swelling and skin and temperature changes (CRPS).(11)
How a cream helps: the evidence is limited, but topical lidocaine or capsaicin over the painful, touch-sensitive area is used by some clinicians as part of a wider plan.(11)
Whole-body care, not mainly a cream
Fibromyalgia
Fibromyalgia is widespread pain that comes from a more sensitive pain-processing system rather than one damaged nerve, so it is felt all over rather than in one spot.
How a cream fits: because topical creams work best on localized pain, they are not the main treatment for fibromyalgia, which responds to exercise, sleep and stress care, and certain oral medicines.(13) A cream may still soothe a particular tender area, and your clara doctor can advise.

Using a nerve-pain cream safely

  • Never put it on broken, cut, raw or burned skin. This is the most important rule, because broken skin lets far more medicine through and has caused real harm.
  • Apply a thin layer to the painful area as directed, usually a few times a day, and wash your hands afterward unless your hands are what you are treating.
  • Keep it away from your eyes, do not cover it with tight dressings, and avoid heat like heating pads over the area.
  • Store it out of reach of children and pets, and tell your doctor before using it in pregnancy.
  • Mild stinging, warmth or redness where you apply it is common and usually settles. Stop and message clara for a bad rash, or any drowsiness, dizziness or palpitations.

Other localized pain a cream can help

A compound cream is not just for nerve pain. It is often a good fit for other pain that sits in one place. If one of these sounds like you, a clara doctor can tell you whether a cream is a fit.
