Complex regional pain syndrome

When a limb hurts far more than the injury should explain, and the skin changes colour, temperature and swelling, it may be CRPS. Here is what is happening, the treatments that help most, and the honest, limited role a cream can play.

What it is

Complex regional pain syndrome, or CRPS, is a condition in which a part of the body, usually a hand, arm, foot or leg, develops pain that is far worse than the original injury would explain. It most often begins after a fracture, a sprain or other injury, or surgery to that limb.(1, 2)

There are two types. In type 1, by far the most common (about 90 percent of cases), there is no clear damage to a single nerve. In type 2, a specific nerve injury can be identified. Both share the same hallmark: continuing regional pain that is out of proportion to what came before it.(2)

Where the pain comes from

The honest answer is that doctors do not fully understand why CRPS starts. After the original injury, several systems seem to go wrong at once: inflammation lingers in the limb, the pain-signalling nerves and spinal cord become over-sensitised so that ordinary signals are read as pain, and the automatic "housekeeping" nerves that control blood flow, temperature and sweating behave abnormally.(2)

This mix is why CRPS is more than just pain: the same faulty wiring produces the swelling, the colour and temperature changes, and the sensitivity to light touch that mark the condition.(2)

CRPS is uncommon and is most often triggered by a fracture, with sprains, crush injuries and surgery being other common starting points. In some people no clear trigger is found. In adults it more often affects an arm, and it usually appears within about four to six weeks of the inciting event.(2)

There is real reason for hope: most people improve substantially within the first year and a half, especially when treatment starts early.(3)

What it feels like

CRPS affects the whole region, not a single nerve line, and tends to combine several kinds of changes in the same limb:(2)

Deep, burning pain and tenderness
A burning, aching or throbbing pain felt deep in the limb, often with allodynia, where even gentle touch or light pressure becomes painful.
Skin and temperature changes
The limb may change colour, feel warmer or cooler than the other side, swell, and sweat differently. Over time, nail, hair and skin changes can appear.
Stiffness and weakness
Many people find the limb harder to move, weaker, or stiff, sometimes with tremor or difficulty starting a movement.
Pain from light pressure
Pain triggered by even mild pressure is considered a signature feature that helps set CRPS apart from many other nerve-pain conditions.

How it is diagnosed

CRPS is diagnosed from the history and a physical examination; there is no single blood test or scan that confirms it. Doctors use an agreed checklist, the Budapest criteria, which looks for the right pattern of symptoms you report and signs the doctor can see across sensory, blood-flow, swelling and movement categories, while making sure another condition is not a better explanation.(2)

Tests such as a bone scan or X-ray are sometimes used to support the picture or to rule out other problems, but they cannot, on their own, prove or disprove CRPS.(1, 2)

Can it be prevented

There are a couple of things that may lower the risk. Taking vitamin C after a wrist fracture has been studied as a way to reduce CRPS, though the evidence is mixed. Keeping a limb gently moving after an injury, rather than immobilising it longer than necessary, may also help.(3, 1)

What a doctor may recommend

CRPS is best treated by a team, and the earlier treatment begins, the better the outlook. The aim is not only to ease pain but to keep the limb moving and working.(3)

1
Physical and occupational therapy
This is the cornerstone and the first-line treatment. Gentle, graded movement and approaches like mirror therapy and graded motor imagery help restore function and calm the over-sensitised nervous system. Therapy is paced carefully, because pushing too hard too soon can flare the pain.(3)
2
Medicines and bone treatments
Doctors may add nerve-pain medicines (such as gabapentin, pregabalin or a tricyclic antidepressant), a short course of anti-inflammatories, and, for early CRPS, bisphosphonates (bone medicines) or a short course of steroids, which have some of the better evidence in the first months.(3)
3
Support for mood and coping
Living with CRPS is hard, and psychological support such as cognitive behavioural therapy is part of good care, helping with the stress, low mood and fear of movement that can otherwise worsen pain.(3)
4
Specialist procedures for stubborn pain
When pain does not respond, a pain specialist may consider sympathetic nerve blocks or spinal cord stimulation. These are reserved for more difficult cases and are done in specialist centres.(3)

Where a compound cream fits

Limited evidence · add-on only

We want to be straight with you here. For CRPS, a compound cream is at most a low-risk add-on, not a main treatment, and the real work is done by therapy and the steps above. Leading guidelines note that, while the individual ingredients are used in nerve pain, there is no strong data showing combined creams work specifically for CRPS.(3)

Where a cream may help is a focal patch of touch-sensitive skin. Lidocaine can ease that localised, light-touch pain (allodynia), and ketamine and amitriptyline appear in case reports, though the evidence is sparse.(4) One retrospective review of a multi-ingredient cream reported that most patients felt good or excellent relief, but that study had no comparison group, so it cannot tell us how much was the cream itself.(5)

Lidocaine targets light-touch pain; ketamine and amitriptyline are used in multi-ingredient creams; baclofen may be added in some cases. The exact ingredients and strengths are chosen by your clara doctor.(4, 5)

An honest word on the evidence

The largest and most rigorous trial of compounded pain creams, in nearly 400 people, found no benefit over placebo, and a recent review of topical ketamine found no clear effect on pain. For these reasons, a cream should not be expected to treat CRPS on its own; it may be tried as a low-risk extra for a localised sore area, with realistic expectations, as part of a plan your clara doctor builds and monitors with you.(6, 3)

Using a nerve-pain cream safely

In CRPS the skin is often fragile and extremely sensitive, so this matters even more than usual: never apply a compound cream to broken, cut, raw or burned skin, keep it away from your eyes, do not cover it with tight dressings or heat, and store it out of reach of children and pets. Mild stinging, warmth or redness where you apply it is common and usually settles. A clara doctor reviews every ingredient against your health, allergies and other medicines before anything is made.(4)
