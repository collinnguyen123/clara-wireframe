Fibromyalgia

Widespread pain, deep fatigue and unrefreshing sleep. Fibromyalgia is real, and it comes from how the nervous system processes pain across the whole body, which is why a single cream is not the answer and whole-body care is.

What it is

Fibromyalgia is a condition that causes pain felt in the muscles and soft tissues all over the body, along with extreme tiredness, poor sleep and trouble with memory and concentration. It is a real illness, and the pain is not "all in your head."(1)

Importantly, fibromyalgia is a disorder of how the nervous system processes pain, not a sign of damage or inflammation in the joints and muscles. The places that hurt usually look completely normal, because the problem is in the volume of the pain signal, not in the tissue itself.(1, 2)

Where the pain comes from

The best-understood explanation is "central sensitization." In fibromyalgia the brain and spinal cord become more sensitive to pain, turning up the volume so that signals which would normally feel minor are experienced as real, significant pain. This is a change in pain processing, not inflammation, which is a key reason ordinary anti-inflammatory painkillers do not work well.(1, 2)

Researchers have also found that a subset of people with fibromyalgia have changes in the small nerve fibres of the skin, but the meaning of this finding is still debated, and it is not used to diagnose or treat the condition in routine care.(2)

Fibromyalgia is more common in women and is the most frequent cause of widespread muscle pain in women between about 20 and 55, though it can affect anyone. It often travels with other conditions, including irritable bowel syndrome, migraine and tension headaches, sleep problems, and depression or anxiety.(1, 2)

What it feels like

The defining feature is pain that is widespread, felt on both sides of the body and both above and below the waist, present for at least three months. But fibromyalgia is really a whole-body experience:(2)

Widespread aching and tenderness
Deep muscular aching, soreness, stiffness or burning across the body, with areas that feel painful under even mild pressure.
Fatigue and unrefreshing sleep
Profound tiredness, and waking up unrefreshed even after a full night, as if you have not really rested.
"Fibro fog"
Trouble with memory, focus and finding words, which many people find as frustrating as the pain itself.
A flu-like, all-over feeling
Many describe it as feeling as though they always have the flu, alongside headaches, gut symptoms and low mood.

How it is diagnosed

There is no blood test or imaging scan for fibromyalgia. It is diagnosed from the pattern of symptoms, using criteria that combine how many areas of the body are painful with how severe the fatigue, sleep and thinking problems are, present for at least three months.(2)

Blood tests are used not to find fibromyalgia but to rule out other conditions that can look similar, such as thyroid problems or inflammatory arthritis. Reaching the diagnosis can take time, which is part of why a clear explanation can come as a relief.(2, 1)

What actually helps

Because fibromyalgia comes from how the whole nervous system handles pain, the treatments that work are whole-body ones, and medicine alone is almost never enough. The foundation is the same for everyone: understanding the condition, moving your body, and protecting sleep and mood.(3)

1
Movement and gentle exercise
This is the most strongly supported treatment. Low-impact aerobic activity, built up gradually, genuinely reduces pain and improves function. Being active will not harm your muscles, and approaches like tai chi, yoga and water-based exercise also help.(3, 1)
2
Sleep, stress and talking therapy
Improving sleep and using cognitive behavioural therapy to manage stress and pain are core parts of care, with real evidence behind them.(3)
3
Medicines that calm the nervous system
When medicine is added, the options that help work on the central nervous system: a low-dose tricyclic such as amitriptyline is often used first, and duloxetine, milnacipran and pregabalin are specifically approved for fibromyalgia. The benefit is real but usually modest, so they are used alongside the steps above.(3)
4
What does not work
Opioids are not used, because they do not help and can make pain worse over time. Anti-inflammatories and steroids do not work either, because there is no inflammation to treat, and locally-acting treatments are generally not effective for whole-body pain.(3, 1)

Why a cream is not the treatment

Whole-body care, not a cream

We want to be honest with you, because it matters. Unlike shingles pain or diabetic nerve pain, fibromyalgia is not pain coming from one damaged patch of nerve or tissue. It is widespread, and it comes from how the whole nervous system processes pain. For that reason, treatments that act in one spot, including creams, are generally not effective for it.(1)

That is why clara does not offer a compound cream as a treatment for fibromyalgia. Compound creams are designed for focal, localised nerve pain, and even for those conditions a large trial of multi-ingredient creams found no benefit over placebo. Selling you a cream for whole-body pain would not be honest care.(4)

If you also have a separate, localised pain problem alongside fibromyalgia, that is worth raising with a clara doctor, who can look at whether a targeted treatment fits that specific issue. But for fibromyalgia itself, the real, evidence-backed plan is the whole-body one above.(3)

The takeaway

Fibromyalgia is real and it is treatable, but it is treated by caring for the whole body: movement, sleep, stress and, when needed, medicines that calm the nervous system. A cream is not a shortcut around that, and we would rather tell you so.(3)
