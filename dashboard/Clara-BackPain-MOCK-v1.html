Good questions

Back pain, answered.

How do I know my back pain is not something serious?

The large majority of back pain, about 80 to 90 percent, has no dangerous cause a scan can find. Serious problems are rare and tend to come with specific warning signs. Our assessment screens for those red flags, and a doctor reviews your answers before recommending anything. If anything looks concerning, we tell you to seek in person care.

Do I need an MRI or X-ray first?

Usually not. Imaging finds "abnormal" results in pain free people very often, and the risk is what tends to happen next: referrals and treatment for findings that were not causing the pain. A scan is appropriate when you have a red flag, when pain has not improved after six to eight weeks of proper treatment, or when a doctor suspects a specific problem. Otherwise the treatments work whether or not a scan shows a "disc bulge."

What are my treatment options with clara?

Three things, matched to you by a doctor. First, staying active and the right exercise, which has the strongest evidence for back pain. Second, a compound cream that delivers proven medicine right where it hurts, with far less reaching the rest of your body. And when more is needed, a carefully chosen pill at the lowest effective dose.

Does exercise really help, or will moving make it worse?

Gentle movement is one of the most effective things for back pain, and it settles a sore back rather than damaging it. clara gives you a pattern matched exercise series, a gentle walking ramp, and physiotherapy guidance, all designed for backs that hurt, so you can start at any level.

What kind of medications does clara use?

clara starts with the lowest risk option that works: a topical compound cream applied right at the sore spot, so less reaches your bloodstream than with pills. When more is needed, a doctor can add an anti-inflammatory first line, a short muscle relaxant course when appropriate, or duloxetine for chronic pain.

When should I see someone in person instead?

Some signs need in person care right away, like new weakness or numbness in a leg, numbness around the groin or saddle area, loss of bladder or bowel control, fever with back pain, pain after a serious fall or accident, or unexplained weight loss. Our assessment screens for these, and if anything looks concerning we will tell you to seek in person or emergency care.
