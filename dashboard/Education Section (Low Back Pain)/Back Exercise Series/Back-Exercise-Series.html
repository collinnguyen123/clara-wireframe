Extension pattern

🦹 Rest positions & the sloppy pushup

This is you if: pain is worse sitting or bending forward, and eased by standing, walking, or arching backward. Walking beats sitting.

Sloppy pushup

Lie on your stomach with your hands beside your head. Keep your lower body on the floor and use your arms to slowly raise your upper body, keeping your pelvis down and your back muscles relaxed.

As advised

Nicely done. Use a lumbar roll when sitting and driving to keep that gentle curve. 🎉
If any exercise sends pain further down your leg, stop and rest. Pain moving back toward your spine is a good sign; pain travelling down the leg is not.

Acute nerve pattern, rest first

🛏 Rest & positioning

This is you if: the pain is mainly down your leg, often constant, and most movement makes it worse. In this acute phase, rest and positioning come before exercise.

Message your clara care team before starting. Because the leg pain is dominant, an early physiotherapy review leads to far better outcomes, and we want a clinician to check the nerve. The positions below are for relief while you wait, not a workout.

4

Claudication pattern, core focus

🧘 Core stability & flexion

This is you if: your legs feel heavy or achy after walking or standing, and it lifts quickly when you sit or bend forward. The long term win is a steady core routine.

Today’s routine0 / 5

Single leg abdominal press

Lie on your back with knees bent and your back neutral, and tighten your abdominal muscles. Lift one leg so your hip and knee are at 90°, then press one hand against that knee while pulling the knee toward your hand, keeping your elbow straight. Switch legs.

As advised
✓

Pelvic tilt

Lie on your back, knees bent and feet flat hip width apart, arms at your sides. Tighten your abdominal muscles and press the small of your back into the floor so the front of your pelvis tilts up.

As advised
✓

Sitting flexion

Sit with your feet flat, about hip width apart. Lean forward to rest your stomach on your lap and let your arms and head hang toward your feet.

As advised
✓

Cat and camel

Kneel on your hands and knees. Arch your back and let your head drop slightly while tightening your stomach and buttock muscles, then let your back sag toward the floor with your arms straight.

As advised
✓

Partial sit up (crunch)

Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat and arms crossed over your chest. Using your lower stomach muscles, raise your head and shoulders just until your shoulder blades lift off the floor.

As advised
✓
Excellent. Consistency matters most here, a regular core routine is what keeps the heavy leg feeling away. 🎉

Healthy back basics (everyone)

Whatever your pattern, these habits from the Saskatchewan “Maintaining a Healthy Back” guide protect your spine between flares. Well balanced core muscles stabilise the spine and support every other movement.

🧍 Good standing posture

  • Chest raised, shoulders relaxed and back
  • Abdomen and buttocks gently pulled in
  • Knees soft, feet slightly apart, weight even

🪑 Good sitting posture

  • Back against the chair, small lumbar support at the curve
  • Knees at about 90°, thighs level with hips
  • Feet flat on the floor; get up and move often

💪 Lift the safe way

  • Bend your knees, not your back; keep the load close
  • Tighten your core before you lift
  • Turn with your feet, never twist under load

🧬 What helps the discs

  • Keep moving, motion nourishes the discs
  • Don’t smoke, it starves discs of oxygen and nutrients
  • A healthy weight takes load off the spine
